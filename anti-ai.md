# Anti-AI Writing Guide

Rules for writing that sounds like a human wrote it, not a model. Applies to the OASAS book and any prose in this repo.

## Hard rules

- **Never use an em dash.** Not one. Use a period, a comma, a colon, or parentheses. (Also avoid the en dash in prose; use "to" for ranges.)
- Never open with "In today's fast-paced world," "In the world of," "Imagine a," or any throat-clearing scene-setter.
- Never use "delve," "tapestry," "testament," "navigate" (figurative), "unlock," "unleash," "elevate," "robust," "leverage" (as a verb in prose), "seamless," "synergy," "landscape" (figurative), "realm," "embark," "journey" (figurative cliche), "game-changer," "supercharge."
- No "It's not just X, it's Y" construction. No "isn't merely... it's." Kill the rule-of-three when it shows up reflexively (X, Y, and Z polish).
- No hollow hedging: "it's important to note," "it's worth mentioning," "studies have shown" without a real source.
- Don't end sections with a tidy moral summary that restates what was just said.
- No emoji unless quoting source material that used them.

## What to do instead

- Write the way you'd explain it to one smart, busy executive across a table. One person, not an audience.
- Start chapters in the middle of something: a moment, a number that stings, a sentence someone actually said.
- Vary sentence length hard. A long, winding sentence that earns its length, then a short one. Like that.
- Be specific. Real companies, real hours, real money, real rooms. Specificity is the texture that proves a human was here.
- Use contractions. Use "you." Let a little dry wit through. Allow an aside in parentheses (the kind a person makes when they remember something).
- Tell stories before you draw lessons. The reader should feel the thing before you name it.
- Have an opinion. Take a side. Bland is the AI tell.
- Cut throat-clearing. If the first sentence of a paragraph is warming up, delete it and start with the second.
- Read it aloud in your head. If it sounds like a brochure, it's wrong.

## Voice for this book

Conversational, warm, witty, story-led, written for executives who are tired of being told to "try harder." Confident but not preachy. The reader should feel understood, then a little relieved, then ready to act.
